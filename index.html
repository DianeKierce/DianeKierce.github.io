#covfefe: Finding Meaning in Usage

In the weeks following Donald Trump's tweet about "constant negative press covfefe," the term "covfefe" took on a life of its own on Twitter. I was curious about how Twitter users were making use of it as a hashtag, so I scraped and cleaned a data set of tweets containing "#covfefe." Using natural language processing (NLP) and latent Dirichlet allocation (LDA), I found two distinct usages of the hashtag along with a third cluster of less cohesive usages. First, a small group of users was tweeting a high number of "#covfefe" with highly partisan, political messages that were either strongly pro- or anti-Trump. Second, critics of Trump used "#covfefe" in tongue-in-cheek tweets or as a clever way to sell anything from covfefe-labeled merchandise to nutcrackers.
This type of analysis tracks viral usage of a term or hashtag on social media and could also be used to track usage of a brand's name or competitor names, discussion of social issues, or conversation about political candidates, among other things.

Predicting Salary from Job Listings

Which features of job listings for data positions in Seattle can predict its salary? To find out, I scraped indeed.com, cleaned my data, engineered some new features, and used decision tree modeling. It turns out that factors including the number of times "data" appears in the job descriptions, the hiring organization, and whether "scientist" is part of the job title are among the most important factors in predicting salary.
I also developed predictor models that utilized only the text of the job listings. These included ensemble methods (random forests and bagging), naive Bayesian models, and hierarchical clustering with LDA topic modeling.
